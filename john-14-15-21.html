John 14:15–21 — The Promise of the Holy Spirit

Dear sisters and brothers,

the passage we have heard this morning comes from one of the most intense moments in all of the New Testament: the so-called "farewell discourse." Jesus is at supper with his disciples on the eve of the passion. He has washed their feet, revealed Judas's betrayal and Peter's denial, and entrusted to them the commandment of mutual love as the foundation of the church. Time is running short. These are chosen words — words dense with promise.

Love first, obedience second

The passage opens with a sentence that might sound like a simple moral instruction: "If you love me, you will keep my commandments." But the sequence matters enormously.

In a transactional view of faith — ancient or modern — the order would be reversed: first prove yourself through obedience, then receive God's favour. Jesus overturns this entirely. Obedience does not generate faith; it is faith's fruit. It is not the cause of God's love for us, but our natural response to his love for us. Like a tree that bears fruit not because it is forced to, but because it is alive and rooted.

This has practical consequences for our daily lives. When the demands of the Christian life feel like a checklist we must complete to stay in good standing, it is often a sign that something has shifted in the order. The right question is not: am I obeying enough? The right question is: where is my love for Christ?

Another Comforter

"I will ask the Father, and he will give you another Comforter to be with you forever."

"Comforter" translates the Greek word Paraclete — literally "one called alongside." In the legal language of the time it meant a defence counsel: not a cold guardian of procedure, but someone who fights actively for their client, who searches for evidence, who takes a stand.

Jesus speaks of another Paraclete — not an inferior substitute for his physical absence, but the continuation of Jesus himself in a new form of presence, freed from the limits of time and space. His earthly mission was necessarily bounded — one place, one era, one culture. The Spirit has no such boundaries. And notice who takes the initiative: "I will ask the Father." The Spirit is not something we earn through spiritual achievement. It is a gift that Jesus requests on our behalf — grace, not reward.

The world does not see — but do we?

"The Spirit of truth, whom the world cannot receive, because it neither sees him nor knows him. You know him, for he dwells with you and will be in you."

This distinction between "the world" and the disciples is not a permanent vertical divide between the saved and the condemned. We believe that salvation has been promised to all of humanity, and that the church exists not for self-congratulation but for witness.

The distinction John draws is one of recognition. The world — understood as the set of values and structures organised without reference to God — does not recognise the Spirit because it is not looking for him. The disciples do, because they have been formed by their encounter with Jesus and have learned to see differently.

But this is also a challenge for us. How often do we, too, walk alongside the presence of the Risen One without recognising him? How often does the Spirit knock while we are too distracted, too tired, too absorbed in our daily concerns to open the door?

"I will not leave you orphans"

"I will not leave you orphans; I will come to you."

Jesus knows the name of what his disciples are about to experience: orphanhood. The feeling of having been left alone, without guidance, without protection, without the voice that gave meaning and direction to life. He does not minimise this. He acknowledges it — and meets it with a precise promise: I will come to you. Not as a memory to be preserved with nostalgia, but as a real, active, close presence in the Spirit.

We too know that feeling of spiritual orphanhood — in moments of crisis when faith seems distant, in periods of doubt or grief or deep exhaustion, when we look at the world and struggle to see in it the traces of a God who cares. In those moments, this word is addressed to us as well: I will not leave you orphans.

The promised communion

"In that day you will know that I am in my Father, and you in me, and I in you."

These words describe something breathtaking: a chain of communion, a circulation of life and love that flows from the Father, through the Son, and reaches all the way to us. We are not observers of this communion — we are participants. And the passage closes by completing the circle it began: those who keep the commandments do so because they love, and those who love are loved by the Father, and the Son will make himself known to them. Obedience is not the price of God's love. It is the visible, daily sign that his love has already found us.

The promise of this passage is as much for us as it was for those first disciples. Jesus does not leave us alone. His departure — which must have felt to the apostles like catastrophe — turns out to be the beginning of something greater: a presence without limits of time or space, a Spirit who walks beside each one of us, who intercedes, consoles, defends, and illuminates.

This presence is not reserved for the perfect or the strong. It is promised to those who love — and even those who love imperfectly, with doubts and failures, are already held within that chain of communion stretching from the Father through the Son to us.

Our response is not to earn this gift. It is simply to open ourselves to receive it.

Amen.
